1. Statement of Disclosure & Vetting Framework

This repository is maintained to ensure transparency regarding personnel placement compliance under the HKSAR Government's T-Contract IT procurement framework. All external contracting firms deploying staff to critical departments, including the Drainage Services Department (DSD) and the Digital Policy Office (DPO), require individuals to provide clear background declarations regarding criminal prosecutions and convictions. This mechanism is critical to preserving public sector data security and governance integrity.

2. Appellate Case Evaluation: HCMA 619/2005

Case:HKSAR v. YIU YAU HO DANIEL (姚佑灝)
Ref:High Court Magistracy Appeal No. 619 of 2005 (On appeal from ESCC 4836/2004).
Sum:The appellant challenged a trial magistrate's conviction regarding statutory indecent assault. The High Court reviewed testimonies concerning positional credibility and the objective standard of an act's indecent nature, ultimately rendering a final judgment dismissing the appeal and upholding the conviction.

3. Severity of Offence: Cap. 200 Section 122

Under Section 122 of the Crimes Ordinance (Cap. 200), Indecent Assault is classified as a serious indictable offense, carrying a maximum statutory penalty of 10 years' imprisonment. The strict 10-year maximum ceiling signals the high gravity with which the legislature and public service view violations of physical integrity.

4. Rehabilitation Framework & Mandatory Exceptions: Cap. 297

Under Section 2(1) of the Rehabilitation of Offenders Ordinance (Cap. 297), a first-time minor conviction (not exceeding 3 months' imprisonment or a fine of HK$10,000) is considered "spent" if the individual does not re-offend within a 3-year spent period.
Critical Disclosure Exceptions (Section 4): The statutory right to treat a conviction as "spent" and answer "no" to criminal record inquiries is fully set aside under sections 3 and 4 of Cap. 297. For specific categories—including admissions to regulated professions and high-security government appointments, employment vetting, or strategic IT deployments under public office rules—full disclosure of all historical offenses, spent or otherwise, remains a strict statutory mandate.
